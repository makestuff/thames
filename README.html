Visualising the Tidal Thames

The Thames is a tidal river, meaning it changes direction according to the time of day. Roughly every 12h, a huge body of water flows south in the North Sea, where it meets a similar body of water flowing north-west up the English Channel. With nowhere else to go, it is forced up the Thames Estuary. As this body of water flows inland, it is forced into a narrowing channel, making its rise and fall more prononced than it is in the estuary. Thus, in London, the narrow river can rise and fall by more than 7m during each tidal cycle. This "range" varies from week to week with the phases of the moon.

The visualization uses a black ball to represent a flooding (i.e rising) tide, and a grey ball to represent an ebbing (i.e falling) tide. If you give it no parameters, it will be a static estimation of the current state of the Thames, but you can also give it a "start" parameter in "YYYY-MM-DDTHH:MM" format to specify a particular time. You can also give it a "speed" parameter. A value of zero means a static picture. A value of 1 means move the simulation forward ten minutes for every second of real time. A negative value will move time backwards. Note the heading gives the current date/time, and an indication of the speed of the simulation. An asterisk after the date means this date is in Daylight Saving, a reminder to add 1h to the (UTC) date in order to get the equivalent in British Summer Time (BST).

You can use the arrow-keys to control the simulation: up/down changes the simulation speed, and the space-bar sets the simulation speed to zero; left/right skips forward or backwards by one day.
